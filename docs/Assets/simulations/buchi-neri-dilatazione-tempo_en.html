Time freezes at the edge

Gravitational time dilation, redshift, and what really happens when a star collapses into a black hole.

M
Mirko Calcaterra March 2026 · 8 min read

Before getting into the details, it is worth pausing for a moment on why this scenario is so useful for understanding black holes. It is no accident that Rovelli and many other physicists rely on thought experiments with clocks and signals: the goal is not to build science fiction, but to make visible something the equations say and everyday intuition refuses to accept. Time is not the same for everyone. And a collapsing star is the most dramatic way to see it.

Where this story begins

As early as 1915, Einstein showed through general relativity that gravity is not a force in the traditional sense: it is the curvature of spacetime generated by mass. More mass in less space means more curvature. And among the things that bend, there is time itself. Not in the vague sense that "time flies when you're having fun," but in the physical, measurable, experimentally documented sense: clocks at the bottom of a tower tick slightly more slowly than clocks at the top.

In 1959, Pound and Rebka measured gravitational time dilation on a 22-meter tower at Harvard. The difference was about 2.5 parts in 10^15: tiny, but perfectly consistent with Einstein's prediction.

In ordinary life, that difference is negligible. Near a black hole, the picture changes radically.

The setup: an astronaut, a star, a clock

Imagine a massive star collapsing under its own gravity. A brave enough astronaut stands on the surface and sends one signal every second to a spacecraft in orbit. Everything is fine up to 10:59:59. At that distance, the signals still reach orbit, perhaps with some delay or distortion, but they do arrive.

Then, at 11:00:00 on their clock, the star crosses a critical threshold. From that moment on, the gravitational field is so intense that not even light can climb out of the potential well and reach orbit. The 11:00:00 signal is emitted - the astronaut sends it, their clock keeps ticking normally - but it will never arrive. Not in the sense that it would take a long time: in the sense that it would take an infinite amount of time.

What the observer in orbit sees

So far everything makes sense if you stay inside the astronaut's frame of reference. But now shift the viewpoint to the spacecraft. The crew in orbit notices something strange even before 11:00:00: the intervals between successive signals stretch out. Not suddenly, but progressively. The 10:59:58 signal arrives. The 10:59:59 one arrives a bit later. The 11:00:00 one will never arrive.

For them, it is like watching a film projected more and more slowly until the image freezes. The astronaut does not vanish in a flash: their image remains there, fixed at the instant of collapse, dimmer and redder over time. Not because the astronaut is literally turning red, but because the light carrying that image loses energy as it climbs out of the gravitational field.

This is gravitational redshift: light climbing out of a gravitational potential well loses energy, and for light, losing energy means stretching toward longer wavelengths, toward the red. The effect exists for any gravitational field, even Earth's - it is simply imperceptible at human scales.

The Schwarzschild radius

The key concept behind all this has a precise name: the Schwarzschild radius. Karl Schwarzschild calculated it in 1916, just weeks after the publication of Einstein's general relativity, while serving at the front during the First World War. It is the radius within which the escape velocity exceeds the speed of light: no signal, no particle, no information can leave that region. The surface marking this boundary is called the event horizon.

  rs ≈ 9 mm   // a sphere only slightly larger than a pea

This is the point that usually creates the most confusion. The event horizon is not a physical barrier. There is no wall, no solid surface. An astronaut falling toward a black hole would feel nothing special at the instant of crossing it - at least for black holes large enough that the difference in tidal forces between head and feet is negligible. The crucial fact is that, once beyond that threshold, no trajectory leads back out.

Two clocks, two realities

And this is where the real tension appears, because the truly unsettling part is not the black hole itself. It is that this thought experiment highlights something true in general: time is not a fixed stage on which physics unfolds. It is a dimension that bends together with space under gravity.

👨‍🚀 For the astronaut

Time flows normally. They send the 11:00:00 signal and, from their point of view, nothing strange happens at that instant. They keep existing, breathing, and moving. For them, the horizon is not a felt barrier: it is a geometric threshold in spacetime.

🛸 For the spacecraft in orbit

The astronaut never truly disappears: their image remains frozen at the instant of collapse, growing dimmer and redder. Waiting for the 11:00:00 signal would require infinite time. Strictly speaking, they never see the astronaut fall into the black hole: they see them approach the horizon asymptotically.

Two observers, one event, two radically different descriptions. Neither is wrong: both are physically correct within their own frame of reference. This is the strangeness Einstein wrote down more than a century ago, and it still has the power to unsettle anyone who really lingers on it.

Why the light turns red

The last piece is redshift, and it deserves its own paragraph. When we say that the light coming from the star becomes "redder and dimmer," that is not a poetic image. It is literal.

Light is an electromagnetic wave characterized by a frequency. The higher the frequency, the more energetic the light - and the more it shifts toward blue. The lower the frequency, the less energetic the light - and the more it shifts toward red. When a photon climbs out of a strong gravitational field, it loses energy. For light, losing energy means stretching its wavelength. The visible result is a shift toward the red end of the spectrum.

In the case of a collapsing star, as we approach the event horizon, every emitted photon must climb out of a deeper and deeper potential well. The interval between successive photons stretches out (time dilation), and each photon arrives with less energy (redshift). The combination creates the reddish fading that observers in orbit would see: the star slowly dimming, without ever fully disappearing, at least in principle.

info

Practical note. In the real world, a stellar black hole is not observed in exactly this way: the photon flux decays exponentially and quickly drops below any detection threshold. But the physical principle is correct, and supermassive black holes at the centers of galaxies have been studied precisely through gravitational redshift effects on the light around them.

One last thought

What makes this thought experiment so powerful is that it does not require complicated instruments to be understood: you only need a clock, a signal, and the willingness to follow the argument all the way through without backing away when the conclusions become uncomfortable. Time flowing differently is not a metaphor: it is physics. And a black hole is not an exotic object at the edge of reality - it is the clearest demonstration that reality itself is structured differently from how we imagine it in everyday life.

Mutatis mutandis, everything that happens near a black hole tells us something about where we live too: inside a gravitational field, ticking slightly faster than those deeper down and slightly slower than those in orbit. The practical difference is negligible. The principle is the same.
