Introduction

While Part 1 covered the foundations (statistical models, classic embeddings, RNNs/LSTMs/GRUs) and the problems that remained even with early neural networks, here we analyze what enabled the real breakthrough. We are entering the Transformer era and will see why it completely changed the way machines understand text.

From this point on, we are no longer talking just about "better models," but about scalability, large-scale pre-training, and the birth of modern LLMs: how we moved from slow, short-memory recurrent networks to systems capable of handling vast contexts, better generalizing human language, and serving as the foundation for tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, and similar technologies.

The goal of this part is twofold. First, to understand key technical concepts like self-attention, scaling, and the differences between model families. Second, to approach LLMs with a technical mindset—viewing them as components to be integrated with retrieval, tools, guardrails, and observability.

We will conclude with the most practical part: the real structural limitations (hallucinations, grounding, costs, fragility) and the connection to the context of this path, namely the geoinformatics field. Enough preamble—let's get started!

The Transformer revolution

In 2017, Vaswani et al. published "Attention Is All You Need", introducing the Transformer, an architecture that completely eliminates recurrence in favor of a generalized self-attention mechanism.

It is worth taking a step back before getting technical. The idea of attention did not appear out of thin air in 2017: Bahdanau et al. had already introduced it in 2014 for seq2seq modelsSeq2seq stands for sequence-to-sequence: a family of models that takes a sequence as input and produces another as output. Classic examples include machine translation, summarization, and transcription, where a starting phrase or sequence corresponds to a new output sequence. for machine translation, where it was used to allow the decoder to "look" selectively at the entire input sequence instead of compressing it into a single vector. What Vaswani et al. did was radicalize that intuition: if attention works so well as an auxiliary mechanism, why not build an architecture that is only attention? Formalized, that reasoning produced the Transformer.

This conceptually simple change triggered a revolution: the Transformer demonstrated that it could scale much better, be trained in parallel, and achieve superior performance on tasks like translation in a fraction of the training time required by recurrent models.

Figure 01: Conceptual diagram of the Transformer architecture. It eliminates recurrence and leverages multi-head self-attention and positional encoding to capture relationships between tokens. (Source: personal creation, inspired by Vaswani et al. 2017)

Let's look at the key concepts of the Transformer architecture:

  • Self-Attention: This is the heart of the entire architecture. In a self-attention layer, every token in the sequence "pays attention" to all others to decide which are most relevant for interpreting that position. Technically, for each token $i$, a query vector $q_i$ is calculated, and for every potential reference $j$, a key vector $k_j$ is calculated—both obtained by projecting the initial embeddings. An affinity score $s_{ij} = q_i \cdot k_j$ is calculated, which is higher the more relevant word $j$ is for interpreting $i$. These scores produce a weighted sum of the values $v_j$ (another projection of each token) to generate the output for position $i$. The formula is:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

where $Q$ is the query matrix for all tokens, $K$ is the key matrix, $V$ is the value matrix, and $\sqrt{d_k}$ is a normalization factor related to the dimension of the key vectors. The softmax function produces weights that highlight the positions most relevant to the query. The result: the output for token $i$ is a combination of the representations of all other tokens, weighted by their contextual relevance. For example, in "She put the glass down because it was fragile.", the word "fragile" acting as a query will assign high attention to "glass" and much less to "she". The model is thus able to resolve the reference correctly. It is important to note, however, that this behavior emerges from training (it is not guaranteed a priori by a single layer) and, in practice, is observed in specific heads rather than uniformly across the entire network.

  • Multi-Head Attention. Instead of a single $Q, K, V$ projection, the Transformer performs several in parallel, hence the term multi-head. Each head acts like an attention channel that can focus on a different type of relationship: one head might capture subject-verb syntactic dependencies, another might capture pronominal coreferences, and so on. The results are concatenated and combined linearly. The benefit lies in expressive power: the model can consider multiple aspects of dependency simultaneously for each token.
  • Positional Encoding. Self-attention, on its own, treats inputs as an unordered set. If we swap two tokens, the attention scores change, but the architecture has no intrinsic sense of sequential position. For this reason, a position vector is added to each initial embedding—fixed and sinusoidal in the original Transformer, or learned during training in subsequent variants. This allows the model to distinguish "the cat ate the mouse" from "the mouse ate the cat" based on the relative position of the tokens, not just their content.

Encoder-Decoder and its variants. The original model by Vaswani et al. is an Encoder-Decoder architecture. The Encoder reads the input sequence, producing contextual representations; the Decoder generates the output sequence one token at a time, looking at both the encoder states (via cross-attention) and the tokens already produced (via masked autoregressive self-attention). It is worth noting that during training, the decoder does not use its own generated tokens as input, but rather the correct reference tokens—a technique called teacher forcing. During inference, however, it proceeds one token at a time. Simplified configurations exist today: BERT-style models use only the encoder (bidirectional self-attention over the entire input, for comprehension tasks), while GPT-style models use only the decoder (unidirectional, autoregressive self-attention, for text generation).

Why the Transformer scales (and changed everything)

So far, we have seen what self-attention does at the single-token level. But the real reason the Transformer upended the field is not just the quality of its representations: it is that it scales. And it scales on real hardware, with real datasets, in reasonable timeframes. Let's see why.

Total parallelization during training

Let's start with parallelization. In recurrent networks, computation is constrained by the sequence: each step depends on the hidden state of the previous step, which makes it impossible to parallelize the processing of a single sequence. As Vaswani et al. write in the original paper, sequential dependency remains only in the decoder's autoregression during inference (step-by-step generation), but during training, even the decoder can be trained in parallel using masks (the entire output sequence "shifted" as input). In effect, if I previously had to perform 10 steps one after another to analyze 10 words, I can now perform 1 step that covers 10 positions with attention. The gain in efficiency is enormous, especially on long sequences. Transformers trained on massive datasets became feasible only thanks to this characteristic.

Long-range without degradation

In recurrent models, connecting two distant tokens requires propagating the signal through all intermediate tokens, and gradients may vanish along the way. Each step is a potential source of gradient attenuation. In the Transformer, any pair of tokens is directly connected: the number of sequential operations required to connect any two positions is constant, regardless of distance, whereas in an RNN, that number grows linearly with distance. The practical consequence is that the Transformer captures long-range dependencies (such as a subject and verb separated by a long subordinate clause, pronominal coreferences, or long-distance agreement) much more reliably, without the need for additional mechanisms.

In summary, the Transformer calculates global dependencies in one shot. This leads to a natural ability to capture long-term relationships (e.g., identifying the subject of a distant verb, long-distance agreement, etc.) much better than RNNs. Formally, in Transformers, the number of operations required to connect any two positions is constant, while in an RNN, it is proportional to their distance in the sequence.

Complexity and trade-offs

Nothing comes for free. The computational cost of self-attention grows as $O(N^2)$ with sequence length because every token is compared with every other token: the attention score matrix is $N \times N$, and it must be calculated, normalized, and multiplied by the values. This quadratic complexity manifests in both time and memory, becoming prohibitive for very long sequences. An RNN has $O(N)$ complexity per step, so in principle, it is more efficient for long sequences, but it pays the price of serialization. For typical NLP application lengths (on the order of thousands of tokens), the trade-off heavily favors the Transformer due to parallel execution. For truly long sequences (tens or hundreds of thousands of tokens), the bottleneck becomes a significant issue; this is why many sparse attention approaches have emerged over the years, such as Sparse Transformer, Longformer, and BigBird, which limit attention calculations to structured subsets of token pairs, achieving sub-quadratic complexity while maintaining much of the expressive capacity.

Greater expressivity

Each Transformer layer is not just attention: after the multi-head attention block, there is a position-wise feed-forward network, which is a small MLP applied independently to each position. This introduces non-linearity and allows the model to "digest" the information synthesized by the attention mechanism before passing it to the next layer. The combination of multi-head attention and FFN gives the Transformer a representational capacity that scales favorably with depth: the more layers are stacked, the more the model can capture abstract structures. The community has observed that sufficiently large Transformers tend to spontaneously learn grammatical patterns, semantic relationships, and latent forms of reasoning—not because they were explicitly designed to do so, but as an emergent behavior of scaling.

RNN vs. Transformer

It is worth lining up the points of comparison directly, without beating around the bush.

Regarding parallelism, recurrent models factorize computation along sequence positions, generating hidden states one after another in a strictly sequential manner. The Transformer processes all tokens in parallel using matrix operations. On modern hardware, the difference in training time is enormous, especially on large datasets.

Regarding long-term memory, in an RNN, everything the model "knows" about the sequence up to step $t$ is compressed into the hidden vector $h_t$, with potential degradation as the distance increases. In the Transformer, self-attention has direct access to all previous positions at every layer: the maximum path length between any two positions is $O(1)$ for self-attention, versus $O(N)$ for an RNN.

Regarding complexity, as previously mentioned, it is $O(N^2)$ for self-attention versus $O(N)$ per step for an RNN. The Transformer is more memory-intensive but highly parallelizable, and for sequences of moderate length, it wins in wall-clock speed. For very long sequences, the quadratic term starts to become a burden, which is where the efficient variants we mentioned earlier come into play.

Regarding data requirements, the Transformer has more parameters and greater flexibility, so it tends to require large amounts of data to reach its full potential. Fortunately, the availability of massive corpora and self-supervised training via language modelingLanguage modeling here does not require human annotation: a large amount of raw text is sufficient, from which the model learns to predict missing or subsequent tokens. have made this requirement achievable on an industrial scale.

Regarding the modular architecture: each layer is a series of standardized matrix operations that are easily distributed across GPU clusters. This has made the Transformer an architecture that is simple to iterate on: you can replace an attention scheme, try a different positional encoding, or add a new type of normalization without disrupting the rest. The result has been an ecosystem of variants and improvements that has significantly accelerated research.

Encoder layer schema (step-by-step)

To complete the picture, it is worth reviewing what actually happens inside a single encoder layer.

Input Embedding + Positional Encoding. Each token is converted into its embedding vector, to which the position vector is added. From this point on, the signal carries both semantic and positional information.

Multi-head Self-Attention. $Q$, $K$, and $V$ are calculated for each token, and attention is computed across all heads in parallel. Each token collects weighted information from the others. In the autoregressive decoder, a causal mask prevents the model from "looking" at future tokens.

Add & Norm. A residual connection adds the sublayer's original input to its output, followed by layer normalization. This aids gradient flow and numerical stability during the training of deep networks.

Feed-Forward Network. A two-layer MLP applied separately to each position—the same network for all tokens, but with weights that are independent from layer to layer. It typically expands the internal dimension (e.g., from $d_\text{model} = 512$ to $4 \times 512 = 2048$) and then contracts it. It serves to introduce non-linearity and to "process" the representations produced by the attention mechanism.

Add & Norm. Another residual connection, this time on the FFN output, followed by normalization.

A stack of $N$ such layers forms the encoder. In the decoder, each layer adds a third block: cross-attention, where the queries come from the previous decoder layer and the keys/values come from the final encoder output. It is through this mechanism that the decoder, during generation, can condition itself on the entire encoded input sequence. This is useful in all seq2seq tasks, from translation to automatic summarization.

In conclusion, the Transformer is capable of modeling complex, long-range dependencies efficiently and is highly scalable. Since 2018, every record in NLP has been broken by models based on this architecture. It is the foundation of virtually all modern Large Language Models.

From Transformers to Large Language Models

With the Transformer as the new fundamental building block, the next step was to scale models and datasets to previously unthinkable levels. Two major families of pre-trained language models emerged:

  • Autoregressive models (like GPT): these use only the decoder half of the Transformer and are trained like a traditional language model: given the previous context, predict the next token.

Example: given the sequence "The capital of France is [MASK].", the model (with a mask preventing it from seeing "Paris") learns to plausibly continue the sentence (in this case, with "Paris").

These models learn to generate text one step at a time, and thanks to the attention mechanism, they have a broad view of the context already generated. Training is self-supervised on raw text, with no need for human annotation. GPT-2 (OpenAI, 2019), with 1.5 billion parameters, was already a shock due to its ability to generate long, coherent texts.

However, the real turning point came with GPT-3 (2020, 175 billion parameters, an order of magnitude larger than any previous dense model): at that scale, the model exhibited new capabilities not explicitly anticipated during training, most notably few-shot learning. In practice, scaling language models drastically improves task-agnostic performance in a few-shot regime, sometimes reaching competitiveness with previous state-of-the-art fine-tuned approaches. The model is not retrained, and it receives no updated gradients: it learns the task directly from the prompt's context. This inaugurated the era of generative chatbots like ChatGPT.

In practice, GPT-3 demonstrated that a massive model trained on almost the entire internet in an autoregressive manner can "learn to learn" from prompts, generalizing to many tasks that were previously solvable only with specific models.

  • Auto-encoder models (like BERT): these use only the encoder part of the Transformer and are trained with bidirectional tasks such as Masked Language Modeling (MLM). In BERT (Devlin et al., 2018), 15% of the input words are randomly masked, and the model must predict them by looking both to the left and to the right (thus fully exploiting bidirectional attention). To do this, BERT leverages Masked Language Modeling (MLM) (MLM), which randomly masks a portion of the input tokens, with the objective of predicting the original tokens based solely on the context. Unlike a left-to-right model like GPT, the signal here comes from both directions.

Furthermore, BERT was also trained with an auxiliary task called Next Sentence PredictionNext Sentence Prediction is a task where the model must determine whether a second sentence actually follows the first in the original text or if it is a random sentence. It helps the model learn relationships between sentences and discourse coherence.: deciding whether two sentences were in sequence in the original text, which encourages an understanding of general discourse. For more technical details on this, I have provided a reference to the paper.

Moving on. BERT provided powerful contextual embeddings that, with light fine-tuning, radically improved dozens of NLP tasks (from classification to QAQA stands for Question Answering: tasks where the model must answer a question, often by finding or generating the correct response based on provided text or context. and NERNER stands for Named Entity Recognition: the task of identifying entities in text—such as people, places, organizations, dates, or amounts—and assigning them the correct label.). These models are not designed for arbitrary generation (they lack an autoregressive decoder), but they excel at understanding and producing representations to feed into simple classifiers. BERT was a watershed moment: within a few months, virtually every NLP benchmark was dominated by BERT variants. These include RoBERTa (2019, better trained and without NSPNSP is the acronym for Next Sentence Prediction, the auxiliary task used in BERT to help it learn whether one sentence actually follows another in the original text.), ALBERT (2019, smaller thanks to factorization and parameter sharing), DistilBERT (2019, compressed), and others.

Subsequently, the focus shifted increasingly toward large generative models, particularly with the release of GPT-3. The community discovered that scaling model size and data leads to substantial and sometimes qualitatively new improvements. This was formalized in the Scaling Laws by Kaplan et al. (OpenAI 2020): perplexityPerplexity is a metric that measures how well a language model predicts text tokens: the lower it is, the less "surprised" the model is by the data, indicating better predictive capability. decreases roughly following a power law as certain parameters increase. Specifically, they note that loss on data follows a power law with respect to model size, dataset size, and the amount of compute used for training, with trends covering over seven orders of magnitude. No signs of saturation appeared on the horizon: bigger is better. In other words, if you double the parameters (and proportionally the data and compute), the error drops in a predictable way (albeit with slightly diminishing returns). This encouraged a race toward larger model sizes: GPT-3 with 175B was followed by even larger models like Megatron-Turing NLG (Microsoft-Nvidia, 530B, 2021) and various Chinese models with 200+ billion parameters.

Figure 03: Comparison between Transformers and LSTMs as parameters and context length increase. The core message is that Transformers continue to benefit from longer contexts, while LSTMs tend to saturate earlier. (Source: Kaplan et al., 2020)

However, in 2022, a DeepMind study (Chinchilla by Hoffmann et al.) recalibrated this perspective: it was discovered that many LLMs were under-trained relative to their size. Chinchilla (70B parameters) was trained with 4 times more data than Gopher (a model much larger than GPT-3), showing superior performance to these larger models despite having less than a third of the parameters, because it had utilized its compute budget more effectively.

In practice, it is not just about increasing parameters; it is about ensuring you have enough data to train them well. This introduces a new problem: once you have scraped all the text available on the internet, you need new (synthetic?) or multimodal data to keep pushing training forward. That is where the research frontier has shifted.

Emergent abilities

One of the most discussed phenomena in the last five years is the appearance of capabilities that seem entirely absent in smaller models. Wei et al. (2022) cataloged and precisely defined these emergent abilities: an ability is emergent if it is not present in smaller models but appears in larger ones, meaning it cannot be predicted simply by extrapolating performance at a smaller scale.

The intriguing part is not that large models are better—that was certainly expected. The point is that emergent abilities have performance close to random in smaller models, and their appearance cannot be predicted by extrapolating scaling laws based on smaller models. They work well above a certain threshold and remain essentially random below it; it seems, therefore, that there is not a gradual improvement, but rather a leap.

Consider concrete examples. In arithmetic and multi-step reasoning tasks, models show random performance across many orders of magnitude of compute or parameters, only to suddenly achieve robust accuracy beyond a certain scaling threshold. A particularly well-studied case is chain-of-thought prompting: the technique of guiding the model to produce a chain of intermediate steps before the final answer. Researchers discovered in 2022 that chain-of-thought prompting has no positive effect on small models and only yields performance gains in models with approximately 100 billion parameters. In smaller models, the chains produced are fluent but illogical, and they actually worsen results compared to direct prompting.

However, the debate is not settled. Schaeffer et al. (2023) proposed an alternative explanation, which they called the "mirage hypothesis": emergent abilities appear due to the researcher's choice of metric, not because of fundamental changes in the model's behavior with scale. Non-linear or discontinuous metrics produce apparent emergent abilities, while linear or continuous metrics produce smooth, continuous, and predictable changes. In essence, the discontinuity might lie in the measurement metric, not in the model itself.

The issue remains open and is a very active area of literature. What can be said with reasonable certainty is that very large models exhibit qualitatively different behaviors from smaller ones in practice: they follow complex instructions (instruction following), plan multi-step reasoning, and interface with external tools if properly instructed. Whether this property is "truly" emergent in the physical sense of the term, an artifact of measurement, or something in between, is one of the most interesting knots in current research. What I do know is that a small model like Google's Gemma has absolutely no difficulty with reasoning. Therefore, I believe that today these open models (not fully open source, given that we do not know the true architecture and optimization techniques) are highly performant and have reached high levels of capability. Consequently, it is clear that this mythical threshold has been lowered significantly.

Are we sure that "bigger = better?"

The drive to scale has led to enormous progress, but it has not solved every problem. Beyond the computational cost (training GPT-3 cost millions of dollars, and GPT-4 significantly more), there are practical limitations that are difficult to ignore. Very large models are difficult to update, deploy, and run in production with acceptable latency. Some fragilities persist even as models grow: the tendency to hallucinate, various systematic biases, and the difficulty in saying "I don't know." In fact, very large models often hallucinate more convincingly, which makes errors harder to detect.

As we saw with Chinchilla, there is no need to scale parameters indefinitely if they cannot be fed with adequate data. Today, a significant portion of research is focused on a different idea: efficient scaling. This involves better data selection, architectures specialized for long contexts (Transformers with sparse or partially recurrent attention), and, above all, smaller but specialized models, the so-called Small Language Models.

A benchmark example in the field is Alpaca (Stanford, 2023): a model with only 7 billion parameters, derived from Meta's LLaMA through fine-tuning on 52,000 instruction-following examples generated by a model like GPT-3.5 (text-davinci-003). The cost of the entire process (data generation + fine-tuning) was less than 600 dollars, yet in a blind evaluation between Alpaca 7B and text-davinci-003, the two models performed very similarly: 90 preferences for Alpaca versus 89 for text-davinci-003. Alpaca remains an important case study: not so much for its absolute performance, but for what it demonstrates about the relationship between model size and practical utility when quality fine-tuning data is available.

In my view, small models will prevail in the future. The most recent confirmation is Gemma 4 (Google DeepMind, April 2026): four open-weight models built on the same research as Gemini 3, released under an Apache 2.0 license, with sizes ranging from a few billion parameters up to 31B. The 31B model achieves an ELO score of approximately 1452 on Arena AI, ranking third globally among open models, yet it is the smallest among those on the podium. The 26B MoE variant achieves similar performance with only 3.8 billion active parameters per token—a remarkable difference in efficiency. The point is not the number of parameters; it is what you can achieve with the ones you activate.

In conclusion, from 2018 to today, we have moved from the ~110 million parameter Transformer of BERT-base to LLMs with hundreds of billions of parameters, and then back toward smaller but better-trained models. This trajectory has not gone in just one direction. It has unlocked latent capabilities and opened up new applications, but it has also highlighted problems of alignment (how to avoid toxic output, ensure truthfulness) and deployment efficiency. This brings us to the current context, where an LLM is rarely used in isolation: it is integrated into larger systems to make it reliable, updatable, and useful in real-world application contexts.

Conclusion

If in part 1 we left NLP at a point where models were finally beginning to "remember," here we have seen the real change of pace: the Transformer did not just improve performance; it changed the very way we design language-based systems. From there came BERT, GPT, scaling laws, emergent abilities, and also a new awareness: more power does not automatically mean more reliability.

Today, in fact, a useful LLM is almost never an isolated object. It is a component within a larger system, complete with retrieval, tools, controls, and observability. And this is perhaps the most important lesson to take home: understanding LLMs does not just mean knowing how they generate text, but also knowing when to trust them, when not to, and how to build something around them that truly holds up in the real world.
