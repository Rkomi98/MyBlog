## Abstract

Before diving in, it is worth pausing for a moment to consider why this scenario is so useful for understanding black holes. It is no coincidence that Rovelli, Hawking, and many other physicists resort to *"thought experiments"* involving clocks and signals: the point is to demonstrate something that the equations dictate but that our everyday intuition refuses to accept. **Time is not the same for everyone.** And a collapsing star is the most spectacular way to realize this.

## Where this story comes from

As early as 1915, with general relativity, Einstein showed that gravity is not a force in the traditional sense: it is the curvature of spacetime generated by mass. The more mass in a small space, the greater the curvature. And among the things that get curved is time itself.

To better understand this, the following example (which I found in Hawking's book) is often used: clocks at the bottom of a tower tick slightly slower than those at the top.

> In 1959, Pound and Rebka measured gravitational time dilation on a 22-meter tower at Harvard. The difference was about 2.5 parts in $10^{15}$: minuscule, but perfectly in line with Einstein's predictions.

In ordinary life, this difference is irrelevant. Near a black hole, however, the picture changes radically.

## The scenario: an astronaut, a star, and a clock

Imagine a massive star collapsing in on itself. A daring astronaut is on the surface, sending a signal every second toward their orbiting spacecraft.

Up to a certain point, everything works as expected: the signals arrive, perhaps with a slight delay, but they arrive. Then, the star crosses the critical threshold associated with its Schwarzschild radius. From that point on, light can no longer escape outward. Everything is normal up until `10:59:59`. At that distance, the signals arrive in orbit with some delay or distortion, but they arrive.

Then, at `11:00:00` on their clock, the star crosses a critical threshold. From that moment on, the gravitational field is so intense that not even light can escape outward to reach the spacecraft in orbit. The `11:00:00` signal is emitted. The astronaut sends it, their clock ticks normally, but it will never arrive.

## What the observer in orbit sees

So far, we have looked at what happens from the astronaut's perspective. But now, let's shift the viewpoint to the spacecraft. The crew in orbit notices something strange even before `11:00:00`: the intervals between one signal and the next grow longer. Not suddenly, but progressively. The `10:59:58` signal arrives. The `10:59:59` signal arrives a bit later. The `11:00:00` signal will never arrive.

The result is that the astronaut is not seen "falling into" the black hole in the intuitive sense of the term. To the observers, it is like watching a film being played in slow motion, until the image freezes. The astronaut does not disappear in a flash: their image remains there, motionless at the moment of collapse, growing dimmer and redder. Not because they are turning red, but because the light carrying their image loses energy as it climbs out of the gravitational field.

This is **gravitational redshift**: light climbing out of a gravitational well loses energy, and for light, losing energy means stretching toward longer wavelengths—toward the red end of the spectrum. This applies to any gravitational field, including Earth's, though it is imperceptible at human scales.

## The Schwarzschild radius

The central concept behind all of this has a precise name: **the Schwarzschild radius**. Karl Schwarzschild calculated it in 1916, just weeks after Einstein published his general theory of relativity, while serving at the front during World War I. It is the radius within which the escape velocity exceeds the speed of light: no signal, no particle, and no information can escape from that region. The surface that marks this boundary is called the event horizon.

The boundary that matters in this reasoning is the event horizon, which is linked to the Schwarzschild radius:

$$
r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}
$$

where:

- $G$ is the gravitational constant $(6.674 \times 10^{-11} N\cdot m^2/kg^2)$
- $M$ is the mass of the object $(kg)$
- $c$ is the speed of light $(2.998 \times 10^8 m/s)$

If all the mass of the Sun were compressed to within about 3 km, the Sun would become a black hole. For Earth, the Schwarzschild radius would be about 9 millimeters.

The important point is that the event horizon **is not a material wall**. There is no wall to bump into. It is a geometric boundary in spacetime: beyond that threshold, no trajectory can lead back to the outside.

This is a point that usually causes a lot of confusion, so I want to repeat it clearly. The event horizon **is not a physical barrier**. There is no wall, no solid surface. An astronaut falling toward a black hole would feel nothing special at the moment they crossed it (at least for black holes large enough that the difference in forces between their head and feet is negligible). The fact is that, once past that threshold, there is no trajectory that leads back.

## Two observers, two correct descriptions

This is where things get tricky, because the truly unsettling part is not the black hole itself. It is that this thought experiment highlights something that is generally true: time is not the independent variable that physics is based on. It is a dimension that deforms along with space under the influence of gravity.

- **For the astronaut:** time flows normally, and crossing the horizon is nothing special from a local perspective.
- **For the distant observer:** the final signal never arrives, and the image of the astronaut remains frozen, appearing closer and closer to the edge.

The two descriptions do not contradict each other; they belong to two different reference frames and are both consistent with general relativity.

## Why light turns red

The final piece of the puzzle is **redshift**, and it deserves a section of its own. When we say that light coming from the star becomes "redder and dimmer," it is not a poetic image (even though you know I love being poetic); it is literal.

Light is an electromagnetic wave characterized by a frequency. The higher the frequency, the more energetic the light and the more it shifts toward the blue. The lower the frequency, the less energetic it is and the more it shifts toward the red. When a photon climbs out of an intense gravitational field, it loses energy. For light, losing energy means stretching its wavelength. The visible result is a shift toward the red end of the spectrum.

In the case of a collapsing star, as we approach the event horizon, every emitted photon must climb out of an increasingly deep potential well. The interval between successive photons stretches (time dilation), and each photon arrives with less energy (redshift). The combination produces that red fade-out that observers in orbit would see: the star slowly dimming, without ever completely disappearing, at least in principle.

> In the real world, a stellar black hole is not observable in this way: the flux of photons decreases exponentially and quickly falls below any detection threshold. However, the physical principle is exact, and supermassive black holes at the centers of galaxies have been observed precisely through the effects of gravitational redshift on the light surrounding them.

## A final thought

The fact that time flows differently is not just a metaphor I have often used in my poems; it is a phenomenon well explained by physics. And a black hole is nothing more than the most fascinating demonstration that reality itself is structurally different from how we imagine it when we leave the house in the morning.

Mutatis mutandis, everything that happens near a black hole tells us something about where we are, too: we are in a gravitational field, moving slightly faster than those living on the plains, and slightly slower than those orbiting in space. In practice, the difference is negligible. But the principle remains the same.
