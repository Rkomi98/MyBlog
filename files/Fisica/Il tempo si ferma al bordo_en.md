## Abstract

When we talk about black holes, one of the most counterintuitive ideas is not that they "swallow everything," but that time stops flowing at the same rate for all observers. This thought experiment is designed to visualize exactly that: for the person falling in, everything continues normally; for the person observing from afar, the signals become increasingly sparse, weaker, and redder.

## Where the paradox comes from

General relativity tells us that gravity is not a force in the classical sense, but rather the curvature of spacetime produced by mass. This means that it is not just trajectories and orbits that bend; time itself is deformed.

This effect already exists on Earth. Clocks lower in a gravitational field run slightly slower than those higher up. In 1959, Pound and Rebka measured this very effect on a tower about 22 meters high at Harvard, experimentally confirming a prediction Einstein had made just a few decades earlier.

In everyday life, the difference is minuscule. Near a black hole, however, it becomes dominant.

## The thought scenario

Imagine a very massive star collapsing in on itself. An astronaut is on the surface, sending a light signal every second to a spacecraft in orbit.

Up to a certain point, everything works as expected: the signals arrive—perhaps with a slight delay, but they arrive. Then, the star crosses the critical threshold associated with its Schwarzschild radius. From that point on, light can no longer escape outward.

For the astronaut, nothing "magical" happens the moment they cross that threshold. Their clock keeps ticking normally, they continue to exist, and they continue to send the `11:00:00` signal. But for those observing from afar, that signal will never arrive.

## What the observer in orbit sees

From the spacecraft's perspective, the intervals between one signal and the next begin to stretch. It is not a sudden effect, but a progressive one: each pulse takes a little longer than the previous one to reach the remote observer.

In parallel, the light loses energy as it climbs out of the gravitational well. This is **gravitational redshift**: a lower frequency means redder light and, at the same time, a fainter image.

The result is that the astronaut is not seen "falling into" the black hole in the intuitive sense of the term. Their image appears to freeze closer and closer to the event horizon, gradually fading away.

## The Schwarzschild radius

The boundary that matters in this reasoning is the event horizon, linked to the Schwarzschild radius:

$$
r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}
$$

where:

- $G$ is the gravitational constant
- $M$ is the mass of the object
- $c$ is the speed of light

If all the mass of the Sun were compressed to within about 3 km, the Sun would become a black hole. For Earth, the Schwarzschild radius would be about 9 millimeters.

The important point is that the event horizon **is not a physical wall**. There is no wall to hit. It is a geometric boundary in spacetime: beyond that threshold, no trajectory can carry information back to the outside.

## Two observers, two correct descriptions

This is the heart of the problem. We have two different accounts of the same phenomenon:

- **For the astronaut:** time flows normally, and crossing the horizon is nothing special from a local perspective.
- **For the distant observer:** the final signal never arrives, and the astronaut's image remains frozen closer and closer to the edge.

The two descriptions do not contradict each other: they belong to two different reference frames and are both consistent with general relativity.

## Why this thought experiment matters

Black holes are extreme, but the principle behind them is not. We, too, already live within a gravitational field and, therefore, within a time that does not flow perfectly uniformly relative to every other possible observer.

The value of this thought experiment lies here: it shows that time is not a static backdrop against which "things happen," but a dynamic part of physics itself. Near a black hole, this truth ceases to be invisible and becomes impossible to ignore.
